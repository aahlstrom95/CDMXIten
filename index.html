Mega Ofrenda at the Zócalo 🕯️
The entire main square becomes one giant altar — marigolds, candles, papel picado, massive cultural installations. The city's most iconic DdlM display. Open day & night throughout your whole trip.

Gran Desfile de Día de Muertos 🎭✨
THE parade. Giant Catrinas, floats, full bands, 4+ hours of spectacle down Reforma. ⚠️ Road closures will mess with Uber all afternoon — plan to be on foot near Reforma to watch it, then Uber after ~7 PM. Exact date confirmed ~2 wks prior by CDMX govt.

La Llorona — Xochimilco Canal Show 🌊👻
Haunting theatrical performance of the La Llorona legend staged on the canals at sunset. Way more atmospheric than a regular trajinera ride. Heard this is underwhelming tourist trap, but may still be fun.
Through Nov 9 · Along Reforma · 🆓 Free
Monumental Alebrijes on Display 🦋🐉
Giant fantastical painted creature sculptures all along Paseo de la Reforma between the Angel and Calle Lieja. You'll walk right past them on Day 3. The photo opportunities are genuinely spectacular. 📸
🌙 Day 1 · Friday, October 30

Arrive & Hit
Coyoacán 🌺

first night in the city ✨ iconic neighborhood ✨ tacos immediately
✈️ Airport Transfer — Read This First!!

Use only authorized taxis like Sitio 300 from taxi booth inside the terminal. Unlicensed drivers wait outside — do not engage. Pay at the counter before going out. Uber has a pickup zone if necessary, but is not advised as taxi drivers hate the Uber drivers — the official taxi is your safest move on arrival.

Rest of the trip: Uber works great everywhere else. Use it freely ✌️ If you must use a taxi, pull one that looks legit (clean, has a meter, etc.)Be weary of personal/ itenerary conversation in taxis.

🏘️ About Coyoacán
  • One of CDMX's oldest neighborhoods — predates the conquest as an Aztec town. Completely different feel from the centro skyscrapers.
  • Shops and stalls open at 11am — don't expect morning commerce before then.
  • Both museums are about a 10-minute walk from the plaza. You could do both if time and planning allows.
💀 Day 2 · Saturday, October 31

The Main
Event 🎭🌼

historic center, the parade of a lifetime, and canals at night
🎭 Parade Day Alert — Plan Around This!!

The Gran Desfile runs ~2–7 PM along Reforma. Road closures make Uber basically impossible that afternoon. Plan to be on foot near Reforma to catch the parade, then Uber freely after ~7 PM.

Morning
Breakfast at Sanborns ☕
📍 Map
Go early — this fills up fast on Saturdays. Casa de los Azulejos is a 16th-century palace covered entirely in blue and white Talavera tile. The interior dining room is stunning. Order chilaquiles verdes or huevos rancheros. 📸
~10 AM
Zócalo + Templo Mayor ⚔️
📍 Map
The Mega Ofrenda will be in full peak gorgeous mode right now. The Zócalo has been the main square of this city since Aztec times — you're standing on the center of ancient Tenochtitlán. The Templo Mayor ruins at the NE corner are the actual excavated Aztec temple foundation.
⚠️ Heads Up: Political Protests

The President's official residence and numerous government offices face the Zócalo — demonstrations happen occasionally.

~11 AM
Palacio de Bellas Artes 🏛️
📍 Map
Opens 11am Saturday. Genuinely one of the most beautiful buildings in the world — Art Nouveau exterior, Art Deco interior. Go upstairs for Diego Rivera's murals, especially Man at the Crossroads. Entry ~95 MXN (~$5). Allow 90 min. No food or large bags. 🎒
~Noon
Heads Up:Parade Positioning 🎉
As you explore the city, it will likely be shutting down streets to prepare for the Grand Parade along La Reforma on November 2nd. ✨
~Afternoon
Monumento a la Revolución 🏗️
📍 Map
Walk west or Uber several blocks. Take the elevator to the top for sweeping views over Reforma. Open until 10 PM Saturdays. 🌆 Browse nearby streets for craft goods, silver from Taxco, and embroidered textiles. Fonart (the government artisan store) is nearby — fixed prices, no haggling required. There are lots of great restaurants that overlook this plaza as well.
~Evening
Xochimilco Canals 🛶🍺
📍 Map
Secure a driver or dedicated tour bus to take you ~40 Minutes south. Head to Embarcadero Fernando Celada or Nativitas. Negotiate the price before you board — 300–500 MXN/hr per boat is fair, don't accept the first number. Stock up on beers (and gatorades) from floating vendors. When a mariachi boat pulls alongside, pay for a few songs. Saturday nights on the canals are festive and worth every minute. It is very easy to accidentally really drunk here as is everyone is enabling eachother.

🌊 Upgrade option: The La Llorona theatrical show runs at 4 PM at Cuemanco Dock — a haunting DdlM performance on the water. Book in advance via Boletópolis (see Booking section below).
🌿 Day 3 · Sunday, November 1

Recovery Mode
+ Roma ✨

a castle, one of the world's great museums, and the city's best neighborhood stroll
📅 Nov 1 = Día de Todos Santos

One of the two official Día de los Muertos days ✨ The Zócalo ofrenda is still up, Coyoacán is still celebrating — and Museo Nacional de Antropología is free on Sundays.

⚠️ Early Flight Check!

Depending on when you are leaving, compress this day or move Chapultepec to Saturday after the Monument.

~9 AM
Chapultepec Castle 🏰
📍 Map
Arrive right at opening to beat the line and potential heat. A real castle on a hill in the middle of a city — the views down Paseo de la Reforma are genuinely stunning. 🏰 Inside: Emperor Maximilian and Carlota's imperial apartments plus an excellent history museum. Allow 90 min. Closed Mondays.
~11 AM
Walk through Chapultepec Park 🌳
Head downhill through the park — shaded, beautiful, full of families on Sunday mornings. Stop for an elote (grilled corn with mayo, cheese, and chili) from a vendor. 🌽 About a 15-min walk to the museum.
~Noon
Museo Nacional de Antropología 🗿
📍 Map
One of the great museums in the world. The Aztec Sun Stone is bigger and more impressive in person than any photo suggests. Also home to the Crown of Moctezuma. The building is enormous and can be at your own pace. Free on Sundays! Arrive early or buy tickets online. Closed Mondays.
~1:30 PM
Angel de la Independencia
📍 Map
Stroll down Reforma to the golden Angel — the symbolic center of the city. The area is lined with upscale stores and restaurants. Great photo stop 📸 and the natural gateway into Roma and Condesa.
~2 PM
Lunch in Roma Norte 🍽️
📍 Map
El Parnita — casual, local, perfect Sunday energy. Or Contramar for acclaimed fish (arrive right at opening or it's a long wait). Both are excellent. 🐟
~3 PM
Roma Norte + Condesa Wander 🛍️
📍 Map
Álvaro Obregón — the main boulevard of Roma Norte, with a shaded median, art deco buildings, independent boutiques, galleries, and bookshops. Walk south into Condesa for tree-lined residential streets and Parque México. These neighborhoods are the heart of expat gentrification, but have some of the city's best mezcal bars — sip, dont shoot! 🥂
Evening
Early Night / Early Flight 🌙
Budget 60–90 min to AICM from downtown, more with Sunday traffic. Uber is reliable from this neighborhood. Set two alarms.

⛪
Basílica de Guadalupe
Religious landmark + panoramic city views
The most-visited Catholic pilgrimage site in the world after the Vatican — 20 million visitors a year. The miraculous tilma (cloak bearing the Virgin's image) is on display inside. The Cerro del Tepeyac behind the basilica has a hilltop chapel and sweeping panoramic views over the entire city — take the escalator up. During DdlM the grounds are covered in ofrendas and marigold carpets. 🌸

Entry free. Dress modestly (covered shoulders and knees). About 35 min by Uber north of the center. Surrounding area is rough. Best as a morning visit.
Best: morning of Day 3, skip Chapultepec
🏛️
Teotihuacán
Pyramids of the Sun & Moon · Full Day Trip
The Pyramid of the Sun is the third-largest pyramid on earth. The Avenue of the Dead is genuinely awe-inspiring. A bucket-list experience.

Most organized tours include a stop at an agave plantation and pulque tasting along the way, which adds about 1.5 hours and is worth it. Tours run ~$30–60 USD per person. Go early — it's open desert, high altitude sun, no shade.

Replaces nearly a full day and suggest getting a private driver — plan accordingly
💅 Know Before You Go

Cultural Tips + Safety

  • Never leave your phone on a restaurant table — snatch-and-run is the most common tourist theft in CDMX.
  • This is why so many of the best restaurants have seating on upper floors or interior patios — it's deliberate design to protect customers.
  • While in public keep it in a front pocket or bag. Take it out, use it, put it away. Same goes for cameras and sunglasses.
  • CDMX has a public early warning system (SASMEX) — a loud siren gives you ~60–90 sec before shaking. If you hear it: calmly exit and move to open space.
  • Look for green "Punto de Reunión" signs on building exteriors near your hotel and the places you visit. Those are the city's designated earthquake assembly points. Note where they are when you arrive somewhere new.
  • Most quakes are minor and brief. Locals won't panic and neither should you 🤙
  • Not Halloween. A sacred pre-Columbian tradition about souls of the dead returning to visit the living. It's a celebration, not mourning — eat, drink, and enjoy it. 🌺
  • Key ofrenda items: cempasúchil (marigolds) to guide the spirits, a photo of the deceased, their favorite food, candles, and copal incense.
  • If you see a family ofrenda on the street, ask before photographing — most people are happy to explain it.
  • Face painting as a Catrina is fully welcomed. The Catrina figure was created by Mexican artist José Guadalupe Posada.
  • Must-eat this season: pan de muerto (sweet marigold bread, only available now), sugar skulls, tamales, atole (a warm masa drink). 🍞
  • Uber: Works reliably throughout CDMX, but often need to find WiFi to book. Best for nights, longer distances, and the airport return on Nov 2.
  • Airport arrival = Suggest Sitio 300 authorized taxi. Establish price upfront inside the terminal before going outside.
  • Metro: ~5 MXN per ride. Lines 1–3 cover most tourist areas. Allegedly "fine" during the day; skip during rush hour and late at night.
  • Street taxis: Official metered taxis only. Never get in an unmarked car that approaches you.
  • Traffic can be intense — always build in extra time for afternoon and evening rides.
  • CDMX sits at 2,240m (7,350 ft). Expect quicker fatigue and alcohol hitting faster than usual on Day 1. Drink plenty of water. 💧
  • Stick to bottled water. Street food cooked hot in front of you is generally safe.
  • Breakfast is the main meal here — chilaquiles, huevos rancheros, tamales, atole.
  • A queue of locals at a taco cart is your quality signal. Tip 10–15% at sit-down restaurants if you feel the service was good.
  • Must try: tacos al pastor, tlayudas, pozole, and mezcal de agave espadín. 🥃

🏠 Airbnb Option
Roma Norte or Condesa
📍 Cuauhtémoc borough, Mexico City
Roma Norte and Condesa are where the gentrified, well-located Airbnbs are. Both neighborhoods are walkable, safe for tourists, and have the best concentration of restaurants, cafés, and mezcal bars in the city. Staying here puts you right in the middle of Day 3's itinerary and gives you easy Uber access everywhere else. Worth the Airbnb premium if you want a more neighborhood feel over a hotel.

Note that Coyoacán (the Day 1 neighborhood) is further south — if your hotel is in Roma/Condesa, it's a 15–20 min Uber to Coyoacán, which is no problem.
